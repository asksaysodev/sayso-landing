The "I already have an agent" objection is the fastest way most agents talk themselves off a live call. The prospect says it, the agent says "oh, no problem, sorry to bother you," and a conversation that was 90 seconds from a real lead just ended. The objection sounds final. It almost never is.

Here is what most agents miss: roughly 7 out of 10 prospects who say this have never signed anything. They met someone at an open house, took a referral from a coworker, or had one phone conversation three weeks ago and decided that counted. This guide gives you word-for-word responses for both versions of this objection, the one question that tells you which version you are dealing with, and the exact thing to say in the two seconds right after you hear it. For the full framework behind these responses, see our guide to [real estate objection handling](/blog/real-estate-objection-handling/).

## The Two Seconds After the Objection Decide Everything

The most important moment on this call is not your script. It is your tone in the first two seconds after the prospect says "I already have an agent." Your instinct is to apologize and retreat. Do not. The second your voice drops into "sorry to bother you" mode, the prospect hears confirmation that you were just another salesperson fishing, and the door closes for good.

Instead, your reaction should be warm, unbothered, and genuinely curious. You are not surprised they have someone. Of course they do. Everyone knows a realtor. The agent who stays relaxed and asks one calm follow-up question reads as a professional. The agent who flinches reads as a telemarketer.

There are two versions of this objection, and your first job is to figure out which one you are holding:

**The polite brush-off.** They want off the phone and "I have an agent" is the easiest exit. There is no real relationship here, just a reflex.

**The loyal prospect.** There is a real connection, maybe even a signed agreement. This person feels an obligation, and pushing against that loyalty head-on will end the call.

You cannot tell which is which from the words alone. They sound identical. The only way to find out is to ask, and the right question feels so low-pressure that even a loyal prospect answers it honestly.

<CalloutBox type="key-takeaway">
Never argue with the objection and never apologize for the objection. Both reactions hand control of the call to the prospect. Stay warm, stay curious, and ask one clarifying question. The agreement question does more work than any rebuttal you could memorize.
</CalloutBox>

## The Agreement Question: How to Find Out If It's Real

The single most useful response to "I already have an agent" is a calm question about whether they have signed anything. It validates their relationship, it carries zero pressure, and it instantly separates the brush-off from the real commitment. Most prospects answer it without hesitation because it sounds like you are being respectful, which you are.

Lead with the validation first. Then ask.

<ScriptExample label="The Polite Brush-Off: The Agreement Question">
That's great, I always respect that. Quick question so I'm not stepping on anyone's toes: have you actually signed a buyer's agreement with them yet, or is it still pretty casual at this point?
</ScriptExample>

Listen to the answer. "No, nothing official" or "we just talked once" tells you the relationship is assumed, not real. That is your opening, and you have earned it without a single pushy word. From here, do not pitch. Pivot to their situation with a question that treats this like a fresh conversation.

If they say "no, we haven't signed anything," your very next line keeps the door open:

<ScriptExample label="The Polite Brush-Off: The Pivot">
Totally fair, and I'm not trying to pull you away from anyone. A lot of folks I talk to feel like they're "with" someone after one open house. Just so I can point you in the right direction, are you mostly looking to buy, sell, or a little of both right now?
</ScriptExample>

That question does two things at once. It lowers the stakes so they keep talking, and it starts qualifying them on motivation, which is the only thing that matters on this call. The biggest mistake here is launching into why you are better the moment you hear "no agreement." You have not earned the right to pitch yet. Get them talking about their move first, then let the value come up naturally.

<CalloutBox type="tip">
If the prospect dodges the agreement question with something vague like "we're good, thanks," do not press a second time. Drop a single line that gives them permission to keep you around: "No problem at all. Mind if I send you a quick market update for your area now and then, no strings?" A soft yes here turns a dead call into a follow-up you can build on.
</CalloutBox>

## When the Loyalty Is Real: Reframe Instead of Compete

When a prospect has a genuine relationship or a signed agreement, do not try to win them away. You will lose, and you will look desperate doing it. Instead, reframe yourself from "the competition" to "a smart second opinion." This is the move almost no agent makes, and it is the one that keeps the relationship warm for when the other agent falls through.

The reframe comes straight from how the best agents handle a buyer who already has a lender. You do not tell them to switch. You make a second opinion feel like the smart, responsible thing to do. Loyalty stops being a wall and becomes something you respect out loud while still offering value.

<ScriptExample label="The Loyal Prospect: The Second-Opinion Reframe">
I completely respect that, and I'd never ask you to walk away from someone you trust. Here's all I'd offer: the buyers and sellers who get the best outcomes usually get a second set of eyes before they make a big move, the same way you'd want a second opinion before a big decision. Has anyone actually sat down and walked you through the three things that matter most in this market right now? If your agent already has, fantastic, you're in great shape.
</ScriptExample>

Notice the structure. You respect the loyalty, you make a second opinion feel responsible rather than disloyal, and you end by handing the credit back to their current agent. There is no pressure, so there is nothing to push back against. A loyal prospect who is genuinely well served says "yes, they covered all that," and you part on good terms. A loyal prospect who has been neglected goes quiet, and that silence is your opening.

The other variation that works on a truly loyal prospect is the satisfaction pivot. Instead of competing on credentials, you shift the focus from loyalty to whether the relationship is actually working.

<ScriptExample label="The Loyal Prospect: The Satisfaction Pivot">
Got it, and that's good to hear. Just out of curiosity, how's the experience been so far? Are they staying on top of things for you?
</ScriptExample>

This one is quiet on purpose. If the experience has been great, they will tell you, and you move on respectfully. If it has been slow, unresponsive, or frustrating, the prospect will vent, and now you are the agent who asked the right question at the right moment. You did not badmouth anyone. You let them arrive at the gap on their own. This is the same principle behind sounding like a consultant instead of a closer, which we break down in [how to not sound salesy on real estate calls](/blog/how-to-not-sound-salesy/).

If they confirm a signed agreement and they are happy, respect it fully. Note the expiration in your CRM, set a reminder, and move on. That contract will end, and the agent who handled this objection with class is the one they call next.

## The Mistakes That Kill This Call

Three reactions end this call before it has a chance, and all three come from the same place: treating the objection as a verdict instead of a question. Avoid these and you keep more of these calls alive than most agents on your team.

**Badmouthing the other agent.** The moment you say anything negative about whoever they are working with, you destroy your own trust. The prospect chose that agent, so an insult to the agent is an insult to their judgment. Stay positive about everyone, always.

**Hanging up without asking about the agreement.** This is the most expensive mistake in the list. You let a real lead walk because you assumed a brush-off was a contract. One calm question would have told you the truth.

**Pitching before you understand their situation.** "I already have an agent" is not your cue to list your closings. Get them talking about their motivation and timeline first. The value of switching, or of keeping you as a backup, lands far harder once you understand what they actually need.

A fourth one is quieter but just as costly: never following up. Plenty of these prospects are perfectly winnable in three months. The agent who logs the details and circles back when the other relationship cools is the one who gets the call. ## FAQ

**What does "I already have an agent" really mean?**
Most of the time it means the prospect had one casual interaction with another agent and assumes that counts as a commitment. In the majority of cases there is no signed agreement. A single question about whether they have signed anything reveals whether the relationship is real or just assumed.

**How do you respond to "I already have an agent" without sounding pushy?**
Validate the relationship first, then ask one low-pressure question: "Have you actually signed an agreement with them yet?" If they have not, pivot to their motivation and timeline instead of pitching. If they have, respect it and offer to follow up when the agreement expires.

**Should you try to take a client from another agent?**
Never badmouth the other agent or pressure the prospect to switch. If there is no signed agreement, offering a brief second opinion is fair and expected. If there is a signed agreement, respect it, note the expiration, and stay top of mind for when it ends.

**What do you say when a prospect is loyal to their agent?**
Reframe a second opinion as the responsible move rather than disloyalty: "I'd never ask you to leave someone you trust, but the best outcomes usually come from a second set of eyes." Then ask if anyone has walked them through what matters most in the current market. If their agent has, you part on good terms; if not, you have an opening.
