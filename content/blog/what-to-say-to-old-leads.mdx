Knowing what to say to old real estate leads is the difference between a database full of dead weight and a database full of appointments waiting to be booked. The lead filled out a form a year ago, you talked once, and then life happened on both sides. Now you are staring at the name wondering how to text someone who probably forgot you exist. Here is the exact language that reopens that conversation, plus what to skip so you do not sound like every other agent circling back.

First, a definition. An "old lead" is not the same as a [cold lead from last month](/blog/real-estate-follow-up-scripts/). An old lead is 6 months to 2 years silent. They are not avoiding you, they forgot the form they filled out and the agent they spoke to once. That changes everything about your opening, and it is exactly why the standard "just checking in" text gets you nothing. For the full follow-up system this fits inside, see our [real estate follow up scripts](/blog/real-estate-follow-up-scripts/) guide.

## What to Say to Old Leads in the First Message (The 3-Part Reactivation Formula)

The first message to an old lead has one job: get a reply, not book a meeting. The opener that works has three parts in this order: a one-line context anchor so they place you without effort, a specific recent reason that benefits them, and an easy out that removes the pressure to respond.

Skip any one of those parts and the message reads like a pitch. Most agents lead with themselves ("Hi, this is me again, just wanted to reach back out"). Lead with the reason instead, and make the reason about their property or their market, not your pipeline.

<ScriptExample label="The 3-Part Reactivation Text (Old Buyer Lead)">
Hey [Name], it's [Your Name], we talked back in [Month] about homes in [Area]. A place on [Street] just went under contract for $[amount] and it reminded me of what you were after. Did you end up buying, or are you still keeping an eye out?
</ScriptExample>

That message names you, anchors the timeframe, gives a concrete market reason, and ends with a yes-or-no question that is easy to answer in one thumb-tap. The "did you end up buying" line is doing quiet work: it gives the lead a graceful exit, which paradoxically makes them more likely to reply instead of ghosting again.

<CalloutBox type="tip">
Roughly 40% of internet leads buy or sell within 24 months of the original inquiry, and most do it with a different agent because the first one stopped reaching out. An old lead is not a lost lead. It is a lead the competition has not re-contacted yet either.
</CalloutBox>

Notice what the message does not do. It does not apologize for the gap, it does not reintroduce you like a stranger with your full title and brokerage, and it does not say "just checking in." Apology and over-explanation signal that this contact is about you. The reactivation formula keeps it about them.

## Old Buyer Lead vs. Old Seller Lead: What to Say for Each

The reactivation formula stays the same, but the "specific recent reason" changes depending on whether the lead was looking to buy or thinking about selling. For buyers, the reason is inventory or a comparable sale. For sellers, the reason is what their home is worth right now.

For an old seller lead, the most powerful re-entry is an updated value, because their home is worth something different than it was a year ago and they are curious whether that number went up.

<ScriptExample label="Old Seller Lead (Home Value Request That Went Silent)">
Hey [Name], it's [Your Name]. A while back you checked what your place on [Street] might be worth. Values in [Neighborhood] have moved since then, and a few homes near you just sold above asking. Want me to pull your current number? No strings, just thought you'd want to know.
</ScriptExample>

For an old lead who explicitly said "call me later" and then you never did, name it directly. Owning the dropped thread builds more trust than pretending the gap did not happen.

<ScriptExample label="The 'You Said Later' Lead (Reopening a Dropped Thread)">
Hey [Name], it's [Your Name]. Last time we talked you said the timing wasn't right and to reach back out down the road. Well, it's down the road. Has anything shifted on your end, or is now still not the moment?
</ScriptExample>

That last script lands because it is honest and a little disarming. Most agents are scared to admit time passed. Saying "it's down the road" with a light touch tells the lead you actually listened a year ago, which is rarer than it should be. ## What to Say When the Old Lead Actually Replies

When an old lead replies, do not jump straight to "want to see homes?" or "want a listing appointment?" A reply after a year of silence is curiosity, not commitment. Your next two or three questions decide whether this becomes an appointment or fizzles back into the dead pile.

Re-qualify before you sell anything. Old leads change. The buyer who wanted a starter home is now expecting a second kid and needs three bedrooms. Find the real motivation underneath the surface answer. There are usually three layers: the surface reason ("we want something bigger"), the emotional reason ("a baby is coming"), and the consequential reason ("our lease ends in four months and we can't renew"). The consequential layer is where the real timeline lives.

<ScriptExample label="Re-Qualifying an Old Lead Who Re-Engages">
Good to hear from you. Quick question so I'm actually useful this time: what's changed since we last talked? And is moving something you need to solve in the next few months, or more of a someday thing?
</ScriptExample>

That single question separates a serious lead from a browser without making them feel interrogated. If they give you a real timeline and a real reason, you have a live opportunity. If they say "no rush, just curious," you now know to move them to a quarterly market touch instead of burning a week of dials on them.

One more move that works on old leads specifically: light risk framing. Not pressure, just a true observation about what waiting costs.

<CalloutBox type="warning">
The biggest mistake agents make with re-engaged old leads is pitching the meeting before establishing the motivation. If you ask for the appointment before you know why they replied, you sound like you are reading a script. Get the reason first, then the ask lands naturally. The full breakdown on this is in our guide to [why prospects don't commit](/blog/why-prospects-dont-commit/).
</CalloutBox>

When the motivation is clear and the timeline is real, transition to the ask the same way you would with any warm lead: tie it to what they just said, keep it short, and offer two specific times. ## What Not to Say to an Old Lead

The fastest way to get ignored is to send what every other agent sends. Four openers to retire immediately:

- **"Just checking in."** It gives the lead nothing and signals you have no real reason to contact them.
- **"Circling back" or "touching base."** Filler that tells the reader this is a mass message.
- **"I'm so sorry I haven't been in touch."** An apology makes the contact about your guilt, not their situation. Skip it and lead with value instead.
- **"Are you still looking to buy?"** with no context. Generic and easy to ignore. Always pair the question with a specific, recent reason.

If your message could be copy-pasted to 50 leads without changing a word, it is not good enough. The reactivation message that works feels like it was written for that one person, because it references their street, their old criteria, or their actual market. That is the bar.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What do you say to a real estate lead you haven't talked to in a year?

Lead with a specific, recent reason that benefits them, not an apology. Anchor who you are in one line ("we talked last spring about homes in [Area]"), reference something concrete like a recent sale on their street or a shift in their home's value, and end with an easy yes-or-no question. Never open with "just checking in."

### How do you re-engage cold or dormant real estate leads?

Send a pattern interrupt, not another generic follow-up. Give the lead a graceful exit by acknowledging they may have already bought or sold, then offer one piece of useful, market-specific information. The goal of the first message is a reply, not an appointment. Once they respond, re-qualify before you pitch anything.

### Should you call or text an old real estate lead first?

Text first for most leads under 50, and call first for older sellers who tend to prefer a voice. When unsure, send a short text and ask "Prefer text or a quick call for updates?" A text gets a faster first reply, but a call builds the relationship that actually leads to a signed agreement.

### How long should you keep following up with an old lead?

Until they transact, tell you to stop, or unsubscribe. If a re-engaged old lead gives you a timeline beyond 12 months, move them to a quarterly market touch instead of weekly outreach. Roughly 40% of internet leads transact within 24 months of the original inquiry, so the agent who stays useful and consistent is the one who gets the call.
