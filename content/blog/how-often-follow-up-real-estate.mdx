How often to follow up with real estate leads is the question that separates agents who book consistently from agents who burn through leads and beg for more. The honest answer is that there is no single number, because a lead who replied yesterday and a lead who went quiet eight months ago need completely different rhythms. This post gives you a frequency ladder you can apply to any lead in your pipeline in about five seconds, plus the one rule that matters more than touch count.

Most agents get this wrong in one of two directions. They either smother a not-ready lead until the lead blocks them, or they ghost a warm lead and lose it to the next agent who kept showing up. The fix is to stop thinking about a fixed calendar and start matching your follow-up frequency to where the lead actually is. This post zooms in on the cadence question that guide only summarizes.

## How Often Should You Follow Up With Real Estate Leads?

Follow up daily for the first three days, then taper to a planned touch on day 7, day 14, day 21, and day 30. After the first month, the right frequency depends entirely on the lead's stated timeline, ranging from weekly for a hot lead to once a quarter for a lead twelve months out.

The reason daily comes first is speed and recency. A new lead expects to hear from an agent, and the inquiry is still fresh in their mind. After two weeks of silence on their end, daily contact stops being helpful and starts being noise.

The number most agents need to hear: 80% of sales happen between the fifth and twelfth contact, yet the average agent quits after two follow-ups. That gap is where almost all "dead" leads actually die. They were not bad leads. They were under-followed leads.

<CalloutBox type="key-takeaway">
The question is rarely "how many times do I follow up." It is "did I follow up long enough to reach the fifth, eighth, and tenth touch where most appointments actually get booked." Plan your cadence to survive past attempt eight, because that is where your competitors have already quit.
</CalloutBox>

## Set Your Follow-Up Frequency by Lead Temperature, Not a Fixed Calendar

The cleanest way to decide how often to follow up is to sort every lead into one of three temperatures based on their stated timeline, then apply a fixed cadence to each. This removes the guesswork and the guilt, because the lead's own words tell you the rhythm.

Here is the ladder we coach agents to use. It is built around what the lead told you, not where they came from.

- **Hot (planning to transact within 60 days):** Direct contact at least once a week. If inventory is tight or competition is high in their price range, twice a week is appropriate. Every touch should move a decision forward, not just maintain contact.
- **Warm (planning to transact in 3 to 12 months):** Direct contact at least once a month. The goal is to stay relevant and top of mind with genuine value, not to push.
- **Cold (12+ months out or no defined timeline):** Direct contact at least once a quarter. This is long-term positioning built on consistency and authority, not urgency.

You will hear the temperature in how a lead talks. "Our lease ends in 45 days" or "we already met with a lender" is hot. "We want to save a little more" or "our lease ends next spring" is warm. "Maybe next year" or "we're just browsing" is cold.

<CalloutBox type="warning">
A Zillow lead who replied to three of your texts this week is hotter than a past client who went silent two years ago. Temperature is about recent engagement and stated timeline, not lead source. Sorting by source is why most agents follow up at the wrong frequency.
</CalloutBox>

The mistake to avoid: treating every lead like a hot lead because you are anxious about the pipeline. Weekly contact with a lead who is eighteen months out trains them to ignore you. By the time they are ready, your messages are background noise. Match the frequency to the timeline and every touch lands.

## The First 10 Days: Spacing Matters More Than Count

In the first ten days, frequency is daily, but the spacing inside each day is what makes it work. This is the 10 Days of Pain cadence: a call, a text, and an email every day for the first ten days after a new lead comes in, tied to one useful idea per day, until the lead responds.

Two spacing rules do most of the work. First, lead with the call, then the text, then the email. The call opens the loop and gives context, so the text and email that follow feel connected instead of random. Second, rotate the time of day across the ten days. If you always dial at 9 AM, you only ever reach one version of the lead's day.

<ScriptExample label="Day 7 to 10 Direct Check-In (Call)">
Hey [Name], I want to make sure I am not overdoing it, so I will just ask you straight. At this point most people are in one of three spots: ready to talk, still looking but not ready, or not moving anymore. Which one fits you right now?
</ScriptExample>

That direct check-in near the end of the ten days does something most frequency advice ignores. It pulls a real answer out of a quiet lead, which lets you set the correct long-term frequency instead of guessing. The full daily breakdown lives in the [real estate follow up scripts](/blog/real-estate-follow-up-scripts/) pillar, so this post stays focused on the cadence logic rather than repeating every script.

<CalloutBox type="tip">
Do not reach out at the same time every day. Morning, midday, and afternoon each catch a different mental state. Spacing the same number of touches across varied times will out-convert the same touches sent in one predictable window.
</CalloutBox>

## When to Change Frequency (You Downgrade, You Don't Quit)

You almost never stop following up with a real estate lead. You change the frequency. When a lead goes quiet or pushes their timeline out, the move is to drop them down a temperature, not to delete them, because roughly 40% of internet leads transact within 24 months of the original inquiry, usually with the agent who kept showing up after everyone else stopped.

Here is the decision logic. Move a lead down a temperature when their timeline extends, when there is no financial movement, or when they go 60+ days with no engagement. Move them up the moment they get preapproved, start touring, mention a firm date, or ask about making an offer. The frequency follows the temperature automatically.

This is also how you handle the "call me in a few months" reply without losing the lead. Instead of vaguely promising to check in, extract a timeline so you can set the right cadence and prove it.

<ScriptExample label="Reframing a Vague Timeline Into a Cadence">
Totally fair. When you say a few months, is that more like 3 or more like 9? I ask because it changes how I help you. If it is further out, I would rather send you one sharp market update a month than bug you every week. If it is closer, I will keep you posted as the right homes hit.
</ScriptExample>

That move turns a stall into a scheduling decision. The principle behind it: every lead should have a scheduled next step and a known frequency, so no lead silently rots in your pipeline. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many times should a realtor follow up before giving up?

Plan for at least 8 to 12 touches in the first 30 days, because 80% of sales happen between the fifth and twelfth contact. After that, you do not "give up," you move the lead to a lower-frequency nurture track. A lead twelve months out is not a lost lead, it is a quarterly lead.

### How often should you contact a hot real estate lead?

At least once a week, and twice a week if inventory is tight or you are competing for the same buyers. Hot leads are within 60 days of transacting, so every touch should advance a decision, like sharing a coming-soon listing or a pricing shift in their target neighborhood, rather than just checking in.

### Is it bad to follow up with a lead too often?

Yes, if the frequency does not match the lead's timeline. Weekly contact with a lead who is a year out trains them to tune you out. The fix is not to follow up less across the board, it is to match the cadence to the temperature: weekly for hot, monthly for warm, quarterly for cold.

### How often should you follow up with a lead who said "just looking"?

Treat "just looking" as a warm or cold signal and move to a monthly or quarterly market touch, but first try to extract a real timeline. Asking "is that more like 3 months or more like 9" turns a vague answer into a frequency you can actually schedule around.
