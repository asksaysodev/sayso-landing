# MENTALITY

# **Book the Meeting**

The goal of every phone call is simple: book the meeting or lock in the next step.  
Schedule it as soon as possible. Meetings scheduled several days out are far more likely to be rescheduled or canceled. Position the meeting today or tomorrow.

## What to do

* Book meetings ASAP  
* Position today or tomorrow   
* Avoid defaulting to next week  
* If the meeting cannot happen yet, lock in a next step  
* If the call goes nowhere, move on

## Example language

“Let’s take 20 minutes to strategize. Would later today or tomorrow be easier?”

# **Don’t Default to Sending Homes**

Sending homes weakens your position and lessens your authority. An automated home search is not a strategy. Most listing alerts get ignored or buried in email. On the first call you do not have enough information to send homes that actually match what the person wants. When agents send listings too early, they stop guiding the conversation and start reacting to it. Your role is not to send links; if all you do is send homes, you’re just another realtor and just another search portal. Your role is to understand the situation.

# **When Someone Asks for Homes**

Prospects often ask for listings early in the conversation. That request should be acknowledged, but it should not become the focus of the call. Redirect the conversation back to understanding the move.  
Example:  
“I’d be happy to send homes. Tell me about your goals. What’s got you thinking about making a move right now?” Then continue asking questions that clarify the situation. Once the situation becomes clear, scheduling the next step becomes easier. 

# **Who Should Receive Listing Alerts**

Not every prospect *should* receive a home search. Listing alerts make more sense once there is clarity around the situation.  
Send homes when the client is:

* A client with a signed buyer agreement  
* A client committed to a long-term strategy  
* A client with a defined motivation, timeline, and a scheduled next step

If those conditions do not exist, focus on understanding the move instead.

# **Motivation Is Everything**

Motivation determines whether a move actually happens. Without motivation, conversations stay surface level. Agents get busy, but nothing moves forward with real direction.  
Motivation determines:

* how serious the move is  
* how soon decisions will happen  
* how flexible the client will be  
* how committed the client is to solving the problem

When motivation becomes clear, the conversation becomes easier to guide.

# **Motivation Reveals the Timeline**

Most people do not start with the real reason for the move. They usually begin with a simple explanation.

* First answer \- “We want something bigger.”

Ask another question.

* Next level \- “Our second baby is coming.”

Keep going.

* The real reason \- “The baby is due in four months and we can’t renew our lease.”

Now the timeline becomes obvious.  
The real reason behind the move usually shows how soon the person needs to act.

# **The Three Layers of Motivation**

Most prospects begin with the easiest explanation. If you stop there, the conversation stays shallow and you are guessing about their seriousness, timeline, and commitment. The best agents continue asking questions until the real situation becomes clear. Motivation usually reveals itself in three layers:

* Surface  
* Emotional  
* Consequential

Each layer reveals something different.

## Surface is the first answer the prospect gives. 

## 

## It explains what they think they want, but not necessarily **why it matters.**

* Examples:  
  * “We want something bigger.”  
  * “We’re thinking about moving.”  
  * “We want to start looking at homes.”  
  * “We might want to get closer to work.”

  At this stage the prospect is describing the situation, but the conversation is still shallow.

  ### 

  For the prospect, it helps them explain what they are thinking.

  For the agent, it establishes the direction of the conversation.

  But the surface answer alone does not reveal:

* urgency  
* timeline  
* seriousness  
* commitment  
  If the conversation stops here, you are guessing\!

## Emotional explains why the move matters

Questions that uncover this layer:

* Why is that important right now?  
* What’s frustrating about your current situation?  
* How is your current home working for you right now?  
* What made you start thinking about moving?  
  Example progression:  
* Surface \- “We want something bigger.”  
* Emotional \- “Our second baby is coming.”  
* Now the move has context.  
    
  For the prospect, it helps them articulate why the situation matters.  
  For the agent, it creates connection and helps determine whether the move is casual curiosity or a real need.  
    
  This layer often separates:  
* people exploring ideas  
* people trying to solve a real problem

## 

The consequential layer reveals the urgency behind the move.

This is where the timeline usually becomes clear. Questions that uncover this layer:

* What happens if nothing changes in the next X months?  
* Is this something you need to solve or are you just exploring?  
* On a scale from 1 to 10, how serious is this move?  
* When does this realistically need to happen?  
  Example progression:  
* Surface \- “We want something bigger.”  
* Emotional \- “Our second baby is coming.”  
* Consequential \- “The baby is due in four months and we can’t renew our lease.”  
  Now you understand:  
* urgency  
* timeline  
* the reason action may need to happen  
    
  For the prospect, it forces them to think about the consequences of doing nothing.  
  For the agent, it reveals whether the situation requires action.  
  This layer helps you:  
* identify urgency  
* understand timeline  
* qualify serious prospects  
* disqualify casual conversations

# **Urgency Creates Action**

Once the consequential layer becomes clear, the conversation changes.  
You now understand:

* why they want to move  
* why it matters  
* what happens if nothing changes

Urgency drives decisions. Without urgency, most people stay comfortable and delay action.

# **The Commitment Standard**

A buyer prospect is not truly committed unless two things are clear:

* a defined motivation  
* a defined timeline

Without both, the person is still just thinking about it.

# **Your Three Limited Resources**

Every agent operates with three limited resources.

* Time  
* Energy  
* Money

Time is capped. Energy eventually runs out. Money follows the first two. Showing homes to buyers without motivation or timeline drains all three. Many agents stay busy but never move forward. Showing homes feels productive, but without clarity and commitment, the process stretches out and rarely converts.

# **Buyer Agreement Standard**

On the listing side you do not have a seller until there is a signed listing agreement.  
The same principle should apply with buyers. A real buyer relationship should include:

* a buyer consultation  
* a defined strategy  
* a signed buyer agreement

Showing a few homes early to build rapport is fine. Endless touring without structure is not, so schedule the consultation as soon as possible.

# **Keep the Calls Moving**

Prospecting works best when you have momentum. Calling one person and then waiting slows everything down. Work through contacts in batches to keep the rhythm strong. Use the Smart List in Follow Up Boss with the dialer list\!

## **Simple rule**

* Call in batches  
* Keep the pace high  
* Do not overanalyze one conversation  
* Move to the next call

# 10 Days of Pain

10 Days of Pain is a reach-out cadence for the first 10 days after getting a new lead. 

Most agents misunderstand follow-up. They think it means “checking in.” You should feel like your follow up helping to move someone from curiosity to clarity. In reality, follow-up shows leadership, consistency, determination, and discipline.

When you get a new lead, reach out every day for 10 days with a call, a text, and an email until they respond. Key point to empahsize from the last sentence: we wrote *and* not *or*. If someone responds to a text on Day 6, then stop the daily cadence and qualify them. The goal of every touch is to end with an appointment or a confirmed next step.  If a lead never responds, you still ‘win,’ because now you have direction \- to reach out in the future or pass to another agent on your team. Uncertainty is expensive in this business\!

Every conversation should move somewhere: a scheduled buyer strategy session or a listing consultation, to name a few. Or at minimum, a clear next action that both parties agree on.

Don’t ask, “Do you want to see homes?” Instead, ask “Has anyone sat down with you to explain the 3 most important factors to consider before making a move in today’s market?” 

That shift alone changes how you are perceived, and this system also protects you. It forces clarity, reveals motivation, separates browsers from buyers, and separates you from other agents

Yes, reaching out daily for 10 days feels uncomfortable when you start. Most agents stop after two attempts. That is why most agents live in a feast-or-famine cycle.   
By Day 10, one of three outcomes will occur:

* You booked an appointment  
* You properly classified the lead  
* You disqualified them to the future

All 3 move your business forward. If you are not willing to follow up for 10 straight days, do not complain about inconsistent results\! 

REMEMBER: You are not a listing portal\! Most agents are only home-senders because their only value is sending links, forwarding listings, and blasting emails. That makes you interchangeable / disposable. 

You must control access to information, and information has value when it is positioned correctly. When you dump information, you are overwhelming the consumer. When you offer insight that relates to a specific scenario, you look like a strategist\! 10 Days of Pain is about demonstrating that you see what they don’t and guiding them toward a decision, not flooding their inbox.

Every touch should:

* Reference something specific from your conversation  
* Demonstrate insight  
* Create curiosity  
* End with a reason for them to respond 

Some things you could share during our outreach:

* Inventory i.e. off market knowledge  
* Strategy   
* Pricing trends  
* Timing advantages  
* Equity positioning  
* Risks & Tips  
* Local news, and much more

# What is LPMAMA

**TLDR**  
LPMAMA is a simple conversation framework that keeps you in control of any real estate call, buyer or seller. It helps you uncover the full picture before you give advice, send homes, schedule meetings, etc. When you follow it, you will stop guessing and start leading\!

**What it LPMAMA**  
LPMAMA works for buyers and sellers. LPMAMA stands for Location, Price, Motivation, Agent, Mortgage, Appointment. It is a structured flow that helps you understand everything you need to create a plan\!

# **Example Buyer Questions**

## 

## Location Goal: understand where they want to live and why.

* What areas are you most interested in?  
* Is there a specific neighborhood you like?  
* What draws you to that area?  
* Are there any areas you want to avoid?  
* How important is commute, schools, or nearby amenities?  
* If the perfect home showed up just outside that area, would you consider it?


  ## Price Goal: confirm monthly payment range, not just total amount.

* What monthly payment feels comfortable for you?  
* Do you know what price range are you hoping to stay within?  
* Is that based on a lender conversation or just your comfort level?  
* If the right home came up slightly above that range, would that still work?   
* What’s the wiggle room?


  ## Motivation Goal: uncover the real reason and timeline.

* Surface motivation  
  * What has you thinking about buying right now?  
* Emotional motivation  
  * What would owning a home change for you?  
* Consequential motivation  
  * What happens if you don’t find a home in the next few months?  
* Timeline  
  * Ideally when would you like to be in your next home?


  ## Agent Goal: learn their relationship with other agents.

* Are you currently working with an agent?  
* Did you sign any type of agreement with them?  
* What has your experience been like so far?


  ## Mortgage Goal: confirm financial readiness.

* Will you be financing or paying cash?  
* Have you spoken with a lender yet?  
* Are you already pre-approved?  
* Do you have a lender you trust or would you like a recommendation?  
* Have you thought about down payment and closing costs?


  ## Appointment Goal: secure the meeting.

* The best next step is usually XYZ. When would be better for you, today or tomorrow?  
* Let’s meet and map out exactly what you’re looking for. Does this afternoon or tomorrow work better?

# Motivation FIRST

 **MOTIVATION**

Motivation is the most important question to ask a potential buyer when finding out their timeline, and if they are serious about transacting 

Work on finding out their MOTIVATION and building RAPPORT by asking about it during the first phone call.  The first phone call and the few that follow are the most important when showing what you have to offer them as your agent.

Good motivation questions to ask:

Why were you looking at this property specifically?  
What got you interested in this property?  
What’s got you moving to {specific city}?  
What's got you interested in moving?  
What’s got you making the move from {current city} to {new area}?

3 main steps of motivation are **Surface Level, Emotional,** and **Consequential.** 

When a client is answering these first questions, use “tell me more” or “why?” to get to the second and third layers of motivation.

The deeper you can get into a prospect's motivation, the more likely they are to transact.  If they struggle to answer any of these questions, or you find that they aren't answering them in the way that you think they should,  it's more than likely that these people aren't looking to transact in the near future.  

The way that you ask these questions is just as important as how they answer them.  Make sure it flows with the conversation so the client feels heard, and it feels more like a conversation, not an interrogation. 

Best way to look at the 3 steps:

Start Surface → “What’s got you thinking about buying?”  
Go Emotional → “What would that change for you?”  
Finish Consequential → “What happens if you don’t make a move?

Good questions to ask to find out more about their emotional and consequential state of mind:

**Emotional**:  
What would owning a home change for you personally?  
What’s missing in your current living situation?  
How would it feel to have a place that’s yours?  
What made you start thinking about this in the first place?

**Consequential**:  
What happens if you don’t make a move this year?  
Do you feel like waiting helps or hurts you right now?  
What’s the downside of staying where you are?  
If nothing changes, where do you see yourself a year from now?  
Is there anything that would hold you back from moving forward?

# Lead Temperatures

**Purpose**

Every lead must live in the correct bucket. Each bucket has a required cadence. When a lead is categorized correctly, your time becomes focused and your pipeline becomes predictable. When it is not, you will be running on fumes

No lead should live in the Appt Met stage as a parking space. If you cannot clearly articulate their timeline and next move, adjust their temperature.

**HOT**

A Hot lead means the agent and the consumer have a clear plan to transact within the next 60 days. There is a defined timeline, real motivation, forward movement.

You will hear things like, “Our lease ends in 45 days.” “We want to be in before school starts.” “We already met with a lender.” “We’re actively touring homes.” “We need to sell before we buy.”

Hot leads require direct contact at least 1x per week. 

If inventory is tight or competition is high, 2x per week is appropriate. These conversations should move decisions forward, not just maintain contact.

Value at this stage looks like sharing off market or coming soon opportunities, updates on bidding wars in their price range, pricing shifts in their target neighborhood, strategy adjustments based on market feedback, rate or preapproval updates, and equity analysis if they need to sell.

Move a Hot lead down if they stop touring, their timeline becomes vague, financing is not progressing, they go quiet for multiple weeks, or urgency disappears. If you cannot clearly explain why they are buying or selling within 60 days, they are not Hot. If you see their name on your call weekly call list and don’t *want* to call them, then they are not Hot

**Every Hot lead must have a scheduled task tied to it. ‘What needs to happen next?’**

**WARM**

A Warm lead and the agent have a plan to transact in the next 3 to 12 months. The intent is there, but urgency is not immediate.

You will hear things like, “We’re thinking end of year.” “We want to save a little more.” “We’re waiting for a bonus.” “Our lease ends next spring.”

Warm leads require direct contact at least 1x per month. 

The goal is to stay relevant, helpful, and top of mind. 

Value at this stage includes sharing off market opportunities, snapshots in their target area, examples of similar homes that sold, rent versus buy breakdowns, rate trend updates, and light strategy conversations about timing and preparation.

Move a Warm lead up if they get preapproved, begin touring homes, mention a firm date, or ask about submitting offers. Move them down if their timeline extends beyond 12 months, there is no financial movement, or there is no engagement for 60 or more days.

**Every Warm lead must have a scheduled next step. ‘What event is next?’**

**COLD**

A Cold lead is 12+ months out, or has no defined timeline. Interest exists, but urgency does not.

You will hear things like, “Maybe next year.” “We’re just browsing.” “No rush.” “We want to see what happens with rates.”

Cold leads require direct contact at least 1x per quarter. 

This is long term positioning. The focus is on value, authority and consistency.

Value at this stage includes sharing off market opportunities, quarterly market updates, major rate shifts, neighborhood changes, new development announcements, investment education, and long term wealth conversations.

Move a Cold lead up if they begin asking specific buying or selling questions, request a lender introduction, ask to see homes, or mention a life event that changes timing such as a job move or lease ending.

# Book Second Appt

# **Showing-to-Second Appointment Script**

### **Step 1: Gauge fit \-** “On a scale of 1 to 10, how close is this home to what you’re looking for?”

**Step 2A: If they say less than 9**

1. **Acknowledge & dig deeper \-** “Got it, so this isn’t the one. What would it take to make it a 10?”  
2. **Uncover pain point \-** “Can I ask, what’s been one of the biggest frustrations or challenges you’ve had in the home buying process so far?” ***(Common answers: haven’t found the right home, low inventory, affordability/interest rates.)***  
3. **Position the second appointment as the solution**  
   * If **inventory**: “Totally makes sense. In this neighborhood, there are several homes not yet on Zillow but set to list soon. If I could connect you with some of that off-market inventory, would that be helpful to your search?”  
   * If **affordability/rates**: “I hear you. A lot of buyers feel the same. What’s been helping is sitting down for a strategy session to explore creative financing options and off-market inventory. If I could walk you through those, would that be helpful?”

***(Both lead to a simple YES.)***

4. **Transition to second appointment:** “Awesome. The next step is to set up a quick 20–30 minute strategy session where we can go over exactly what you’re looking for and show you how we can connect you to those opportunities.”  
   * If they say *“Just send it to me”*:  
     * “I’d love to, but two things:  
       1. I have another appointment after this showing, so we’ll need to set aside time to really go through it.  
       2. We don’t get access to this inventory without commitment, so we’ll need to sit down together to unlock it.”  
5. **Close for the time:** “What works better for you \- \[day/time option 1\] or \[day/time option 2\]?”

### **Step 2B: If they say 9 or 10**

1. **Lean in \-**  “That’s exciting \- sounds like this could be the one\!”  
2. **Offer-writing starter questions**  
   * “What stood out to you most about this home?”  
   * “How soon would you want to move if we got this one under contract?”  
   * “Have you already spoken with a lender about what you’re comfortable offering?”  
   * “Is there anyone else who’d need to see it before we move forward?”  
   * “Would you like me to pull together the comps and show you what a competitive offer would look like?”  
3. **Close toward offer:** “Based on what you’ve told me, it sounds like we should at least draft the offer so you’re ready to go. Does that make sense?”

**Why this works:**

* If it’s **not the right home**, you use their pain point as leverage to secure the second appointment.  
* If it’s **the right home**, you smoothly pivot into offer-writing questions so you don’t lose momentum.

# Intro to ZHL/Lender

**Lender Conversation Script**  
(This works for any lender introduction.)

### **Step 1: Open the conversation**

“Have you had the opportunity to speak with a lender yet, or is that something I can help you with?”

### **Step 2A: If they haven’t spoken to a lender yet**

“Got it. The biggest mistake I see buyers make is that they wait until they’ve found the perfect home before figuring out the financing. That’s a guaranteed way to overpay or lose out.

What I recommend is that we slow things down just enough to get you prepped & comfortable on the financing side now. That way, when we find the right home, you’re confident and ready to win it without leaving money on the table. I can connect you with a lender we trust \- would you like me to make that intro?”

### **Step 2B: If they already have a lender**

“That’s great \- I’m glad you’ve taken that step. In my experience, the only way to make sure you’re not leaving money on the table is to make lenders fight for your business.

I’m not saying you need to switch lenders. But if we can get you a second opinion and a better offer, you can at least take that back to your lender and have them match or improve it. Either way, you win.

Would you be open to a quick conversation with one of our trusted lenders just to make sure you’re getting the best deal?”

### **Step 3: Reinforce urgency and value**

“The reason this matters is simple: if you wait until you’ve found the perfect home to sort this out, you’re at risk of losing money or losing the house. Getting this handled upfront gives you negotiating power and peace of mind.”

**Why this works:**

* Neutral opening question (no pressure).  
* Positions the agent as protecting the buyer from overpaying.  
* Makes “second opinion” feel smart, not like disloyalty.  
* Creates urgency without sounding pushy.
